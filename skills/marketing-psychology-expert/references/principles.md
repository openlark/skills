## 1. Price Anchoring

**Definition**: Consumers' judgment of price is heavily influenced by the first price information (anchor) they encounter; all subsequent prices are evaluated against this reference.

**Psychological Mechanism**: The brain relies on reference frames for judgment; the first number encountered becomes the comparison baseline.

**Marketing Applications**:
- Menu arrangement: Place the most expensive item first so subsequent prices automatically "appear cheaper"
- Original price strikethrough: ¥599 → ¥299, with the original price serving as an anchor to highlight discount intensity
- Three-tier pricing: Basic / Standard (recommended) / Flagship; the middle tier is most easily chosen
- Competitor comparison: Display competitors' higher-priced solutions first, then reveal your own pricing

**Classic Case Studies**:
- The Economist subscription experiment: Digital $59 / Print $125 / Digital + Print $125. The middle option was chosen by no one, yet it increased combo sales by 43%
- Starbucks: Grande ¥33 / Venti ¥36; the ¥3 difference makes the Venti seem like the "better deal"
- Car showrooms: Let customers view the top trim first, then the target model; configuration upgrades then appear "inexpensive"

## 2. Scarcity Effect

**Definition**: The scarcer and harder to obtain an item is, the more people desire it and the higher they value it.

**Psychological Mechanism**: Loss aversion + need for uniqueness; fear of missing out (FOMO).

**Marketing Applications**:
- Limited time/quantity: "Only 3 left," "Last 24 hours," "Limited to 500 units"
- Exclusive channels: "Only available through this channel," "Winter-exclusive flavor"
- Real-time inventory display: "2,837 people have saved this," "17 people currently viewing"
- Tiered scarcity: "First batch of 1,000 sold out → additional 500 → final 200"

**Caution**: Once false scarcity is detected, trust loss is irreversible. Use in conjunction with genuine constraints (production capacity, time, ingredients).

**Classic Case Studies**:
- Supreme: Weekly limited Thursday drops, creating a buying frenzy culture
- Airlines' "Only 2 seats left" notification significantly boosting conversion rates
- Luckin Coffee's "Moutai Latte" selling out on the first day, with buzz spreading exponentially

## 3. Social Proof

**Definition**: People judge what is correct by observing the behavior of others, especially in ambiguous situations.

**Psychological Mechanism**: The herd instinct reduces decision-making risk; others' behavior = social validation signal.

**Marketing Applications**:
- User reviews/ratings: Star ratings, photo reviews, video testimonials
- Sales/service figures: "1M+ sold cumulatively," "Serving 500,000 enterprises"
- KOL/KOC recommendations: Influencer endorsements, authentic user case studies
- Real-time activity: "Someone just placed an order," "Someone saved this 2 minutes ago"
- Certification badges: "Recommended," "Selected," "Top Rated"

**Four Variants**:
| Type | Method | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| Celebrity Proof | Celebrity/expert endorsement | "Same style as XX" |
| Ordinary User | UGC, buyer showcases | Authentic user reviews |
| Peer Proof | Industry client endorsements | "Choice of similar organizations" |
| Certification Badges | Platform/institutional badges | "Recommended," "Selected" |

**Classic Case Studies**:
- Amazon's "Customers who bought A also bought B": The golden rule of cross-selling
- Heytea queue effect: The line at the door is the best advertisement
- Dianping rankings: Rankings directly drive consumption decisions

## 4. Reciprocity Principle

**Definition**: People are naturally inclined to return kindness and favors, even unsolicited small ones.

**Psychological Mechanism**: An evolutionarily formed social norm — owing a favor creates psychological debt, which is resolved by reciprocation to eliminate discomfort.

**Marketing Applications**:
- Free trials/samples: "7-day free trial," "In-store free samples"
- Valuable content: "Download the industry whitepaper for free," "Free access to public courses"
- Pre-sale service first: "Free on-site inspection," "Free consultation and diagnosis"
- Bonus gifts: "Free gift with purchase," "Buy one get X free"

**Key Techniques**:
- The more "unexpected" and "personalized" the favor, the stronger the effect
- Give before asking: Service first, conversion deferred
- Rejection-then-retreat (door-in-the-face technique): Make a large request first, then retreat to a smaller one

**Classic Case Studies**:
- Costco sampling stations: Post-sampling purchase rate exceeds 50%
- Hare Krishna flower-giving at airports before soliciting donations, dramatically increasing success rates
- Dental clinics' "Free oral examination": Extremely high post-visit conversion rates

## 5. Loss Aversion

**Definition**: The pain of loss is far greater than the pleasure of equivalent gain (approximately 2-2.5x).

**Psychological Mechanism**: The brain's neural response to loss is stronger than to gain, an extension of survival instinct.

**Marketing Applications**:
- Emphasize "the loss of not using": "Without change, you lose XXX customers every day"
- Free returns/refund guarantees: "Full refund if not satisfied," "30-day no-questions-asked"
- "Miss it and lose out" scripting: "Miss today, wait another year"
- Trial = ownership: Let users "own" first; the sense of loss at expiration drives renewal
- Renewal reminders: "Membership expiring soon; you will lose XXX benefits if not renewed"

**Classic Case Studies**:
- SaaS "auto-billing after free trial": Ownership makes users unwilling to lose access
- Amazon Prime: Once you have free shipping benefits, it's hard to give up renewal
- Insurance marketing: "You never know whether tomorrow or an accident will come first"

## 6. Bandwagon Effect

**Definition**: As more people adopt a certain belief or behavior, those not yet participating tend to follow suit.

**Psychological Mechanism**: Need for social belonging + informational conformity (others know something I don't).

**Marketing Applications**:
- "Everyone is buying/using" type messaging
- Trend marketing: "The hottest XX of 2025," "This year's viral hit"
- Social media spread design: Create topics/challenges, leverage viral distribution
- Community operations: Let users see that peers in their circle are all participating

**Difference from Social Proof**: Social Proof emphasizes "correctness validation"; the Bandwagon Effect emphasizes "not wanting to be left behind."

**Classic Case Studies**:
- Douyin challenges: One person = no one cares; millions = must join in
- iPhone new release queues: The queue itself is a bandwagon amplifier
- Pinduoduo bargaining: Friends in one's social circle successively joining creates a bandwagon atmosphere

## 7. Framing Effect

**Definition**: Different presentations (frames) of the same information lead to distinctly different judgments and decisions.

**Psychological Mechanism**: The brain relies on the "reference frame" provided by the frame; changing the frame changes perception.

**Marketing Applications**:
- Positive framing: "95% satisfaction" vs. "Only 5% dissatisfied"
- Loss framing: "Not buying means losing XX" vs. "Buying means gaining XX"
- Unit breakdown: "Only ¥3 a day" (corresponding to an annual fee of ¥1,095)
- Comparative framing: "40% cheaper than other brands"
- Attribution framing: "Because you value XX, this is suitable for you"

**Classic Case Studies**:
- Pricing copy: "Only ¥99 per month" vs. "¥1,188 per year"
- Charity fundraising: "Skip one latte a day = ¥30/month to change a child's life"
- Toothpaste ads: "9/10 dentists recommend" rather than "1/10 do not recommend"

## 8. Endowment Effect

**Definition**: Once an item is owned, its valuation is immediately higher than the purchase price would have been before ownership.

**Psychological Mechanism**: Ownership = extension of self; giving up = loss, directly tapping into loss aversion.

**Marketing Applications**:
- Trial = ownership: Product trials, software trial periods
- Customization/personalization: "Your exclusive plan," "Custom-made for you"
- Return barrier design: The psychological reluctance to return after owning
- IKEA Effect: Self-assembly → higher valuation → reluctance to discard

**Classic Case Studies**:
- Bespoke suits: Already measured, fabric selected; walking away = loss
- School district apartment viewings: Immersively imagining life there, bids far exceed budget
- IKEA furniture: Emotional added value from hands-on assembly

## 9. Instant Gratification

**Definition**: People prefer immediate, certain small rewards over delayed, uncertain large rewards.

**Psychological Mechanism**: Evolution has wired the brain to favor immediate rewards; delayed rewards come with uncertainty risk.

**Marketing Applications**:
- Instant discounts: "Instant discount on purchase," "Flash sale"
- Fast delivery: "30-minute delivery," "Same-day delivery"
- Immediate feedback: "Get XX benefits immediately after payment"
- Visible progress: "Your order is on the way," "In transit, 1.2km away from you"
- Gamification: Real-time feedback through points, badges, and levels

**Classic Case Studies**:
- Meituan's "On-Time Guarantee": Compensation for delays, alleviating real-time waiting anxiety
- E-commerce order confirmation page's "reassurance design": Order confirmation + estimated delivery + after-sales protection
- Game daily check-in rewards: Instant feedback drives daily active users

## 10. Authority Effect

**Definition**: People tend to believe and comply with information and advice from authoritative sources.

**Psychological Mechanism**: Childhood patterns of obedience to authority extend into adulthood; obeying authority = decision shortcut.

**Marketing Applications**:
- Expert endorsement: "Recommended by Dr. XX," "Certified by XX Association"
- Industry certifications: "ISO Certified," "National Science & Technology Progress Award"
- Authoritative media exposure: "Reported by CCTV," "Covered by BBC"
- Professional image packaging: Lab coats, laboratory settings, industry titles
- Data authority: "According to an XX institution survey report"

**Caution**: Authority must be verifiable; false authority, once exposed = catastrophic trust collapse.

**Classic Case Studies**:
- Toothpaste "Recommended by the Chinese Stomatological Association"
- Skincare products "Developed with dermatologist involvement"
- Infant formula "Imported from XX country," "XX certified pasture"

## 11. Peak-End Rule

**Definition**: People's memory of an experience is not determined by the average of the entire process, but by the peak moment (the most intense moment) and the feeling at the end.

**Psychological Mechanism**: Memory is not a recording but selectively stored snapshots of key moments.

**Marketing Applications**:
- Service process ending optimization: Specially design the closing moment (gifts, warm words)
- Return/refund experience: The simpler the return process, the higher the future repurchase probability
- Packaging unboxing design: Create a "peak experience" sense of ritual
- Post-sale follow-up: A greeting after the experience reinforces positive memory
- Adding benefits at the end of a membership rights cycle

**Classic Case Studies**:
- Haidilao: Snacks and manicures while waiting (peak) + small snacks upon leaving (end value)
- Three Squirrels unboxing: Box opener + tissues + trash bag, ritualized peak moment
- Disney closing fireworks: Ending the day at its climax

## 12. Sunk Cost

**Definition**: Costs already incurred and irrecoverable influence subsequent decisions, even when they should rationally be disregarded.

**Psychological Mechanism**: Admitting that past investment was wasted = self-denial; continuing to invest = maintaining cognitive consistency.

**Marketing Applications**:
- Progressive commitment chain: "Try first → small payment next → large package later"
- Membership points redemption: Already accumulated 980 points; buying more gets you a reward → reluctance to abandon
- Prepaid/recharge lock-in: "The longer you recharge, the better the deal"
- Check-in/progress tracking: Already checked in for 18 consecutive days; missing one day = a shame

**Ethical Boundary**: Sunk cost marketing should not induce excessive consumption (e.g., gambling-style loot boxes).

**Classic Case Studies**:
- Gym annual memberships: Paid but not attending = waste → buy a more expensive one; attending = all the more reason → renew
- Credit card points nearing expiration → forced consumption
- Pinduoduo "Lottery Wheel": The more time invested, the harder it is to give up
